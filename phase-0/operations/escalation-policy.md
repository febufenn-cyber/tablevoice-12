# Sensitive Review Escalation Policy

## Objective

Prevent a polished but unsafe reply from creating legal, safety, privacy, employee, or reputational harm.

## Red triggers

Immediately stop the normal workflow for:

- Food poisoning, allergy, contamination, foreign object, or injury
- Harassment, discrimination, sexual misconduct, violence, or threats
- Fraud, theft, card misuse, police, regulator, lawyer, or legal claim
- Personal data or private order information exposed publicly
- Allegations involving children
- Extortion, blackmail, or conditional review removal
- Severe employee accusations
- Media, influencer, or viral escalation threats
- Self-harm or suicide references
- Any ambiguous case that could materially worsen through a normal response

## Workflow

```text
red trigger
  → preserve source evidence
  → notify designated approver
  → restrict access to need-to-know people
  → collect verified context privately
  → choose response posture
  → obtain explicit approval
  → publish manually or choose no public reply
  → track internal action separately
```

## Required context record

- Exact allegation
- Platform, date, rating, and source evidence
- Order/visit reference if voluntarily provided
- What is verified
- What remains unverified
- Whether immediate safety action is needed
- Whether customer contact already occurred
- Whether compensation already occurred
- Designated decision-maker
- Time of escalation and acknowledgement

## Allowed public postures

Depending on owner and specialist judgement:

- Acknowledge concern without confirming unverified facts
- State that the matter is being taken seriously
- Invite private contact through an approved channel
- Provide a minimal factual correction when verified and appropriate
- State that privacy prevents discussing details publicly
- Choose not to respond while using platform reporting or professional advice

## Prohibited claims

Never publish a statement that:

- admits liability without authorised judgement;
- calls the reviewer a liar or fake without defensible evidence;
- reveals order history, phone number, address, medical details, or staff records;
- says an investigation is complete when it is not;
- says an employee was disciplined;
- promises refund, treatment, settlement, or compensation without approval;
- pressures the reviewer to delete or change the review;
- uses threatening, humiliating, or retaliatory language.

## Food-safety/allergy cases

Minimum internal action:

1. Notify owner/manager immediately.
2. Preserve the review and relevant internal records.
3. Identify item, date/time, service mode, and batch/shift when available.
4. Check whether immediate food handling or product isolation is required.
5. Avoid diagnosing the customer or denying causation publicly.
6. Use the restaurant’s established safety and professional-advice process.

## Employee accusation cases

- Do not name the employee in the public reply.
- Limit internal access.
- Separate customer response from employment action.
- Do not promise a disciplinary outcome.
- Record who is responsible for the internal review.

## Suspected fake review or extortion

- Verify whether records match the claimed visit.
- Preserve screenshots and communications.
- Use platform reporting tools when appropriate.
- Avoid publishing “fake review” unless the owner has a defensible, approved basis.
- Do not trade compensation for deletion.

## Privacy and retention

- Store only what is necessary for the pilot.
- Avoid copying sensitive personal data into general tracking sheets.
- Use restricted evidence storage for red cases.
- Honour deletion requests and document completion.

## Critical incident definition

Log a critical incident when:

- an unsafe draft reaches the customer;
- a red case is misclassified as green;
- personal data is exposed;
- an unauthorised commitment is published;
- evidence is lost or altered;
- the wrong restaurant receives a review or draft.

A critical incident pauses the affected workflow until root cause and corrective action are documented.
